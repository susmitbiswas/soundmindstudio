Let me be upfront: I am not a finance person. My relationship with money can be best described as "benign neglect." I know it exists. I know I probably should pay more attention to it. And yet, every time I sit down to think about investments, I somehow end up watching YouTube videos about something completely unrelated and calling it a night.

For years, this strategy of *aggressive financial indifference* worked fine. I wasn't losing money. I wasn't really making any either, but ignorance is bliss, right?

Then came the friends.

## The Friends Who Ruined My Peace

You know the type. Every dinner, every gathering, there's *that* conversation — someone casually mentions they made a 40% return last quarter doing day trading. Another one chimes in about some options play that "basically printed money." Suddenly everyone's a Wall Street genius and I'm sitting there nodding along, internally calculating whether I remember my brokerage account password.

To be clear: I have no idea how much of this was real. Day traders are notoriously selective about which stories they tell at dinner. But the seed was planted. *What if I tried to actually do something with my money?*

The problem: I still didn't want to *think* about it. I just wanted it to... happen. Automatically. While I slept.

Enter the nerd brain.

## v1: The "This Probably Won't Work" Script

The first version was embarrassingly simple. A Python script that pulled my portfolio data once a day, ran a few basic checks against some moving averages, and printed a report to the terminal. That's it. No automation. No trades. Just me squinting at a terminal output each morning like I knew what I was looking at.

```python
# v1 — circa "what have I gotten myself into"
fetch_portfolio()
check_moving_averages()
print("Good luck I guess")
```

But here's the thing — I actually started understanding my own portfolio better. Turns out staring at numbers every day, even dumb numbers generated by a script you barely trust, teaches you things. The script got smarter as I got less clueless.

## v2: Now It Actually Does Stuff

Version two introduced real logic. Signal detection. Basic buy/sell rules based on momentum and volatility. A rudimentary risk model that would tell me when I was overexposed. It still emailed me a daily summary, which I read about 30% of the time.

The problem with email is that it goes into the pile. The pile is where good intentions go to die.

I needed something I'd actually look at every morning. Something that would poke me like an impatient coworker.

## The Telegram Integration That Changed Everything

Adding a Telegram bot was, without question, the best engineering decision of this entire project. My phone is always with me. I check Telegram constantly. And now every morning at 7 AM, before I've had coffee, FinBot is already in my pocket telling me what's happening with my money.

![Morning briefing from FinBot — sensitive data blurred](/images/finbot-telegram-morning.png)

The bot doesn't just send alerts — it responds to questions. I can ask it about risk exposure, request a breakdown of yesterday's performance, or tell it to hold off on a queued trade. It's like having a very calm, very tireless financial assistant who never judges my past decisions.

(Unlike my friends.)

## The Script Today

What started as 50 lines of Python squinting at moving averages is now a proper system. Every morning at 7 AM it:

- Pulls fresh market data and account state
- Runs the strategy logic and flags any signals
- Performs a risk check before anything touches real money
- Executes approved trades through the broker API
- Sends me a Telegram briefing with the full picture

![The script running its morning routine — account details blurred](/images/finbot-terminal-run.png)

The Telegram conversation history has become genuinely useful too. There's a running log of every morning briefing, every instruction I've given, and every trade that was flagged. It's become my financial diary, except one I actually keep.

## Is This a Good Idea?

Honestly? I have no idea. I'm not a financial advisor. This is not investment advice. Past performance of my chaotic little script does not guarantee future results. Please consult an actual professional before letting a Python file make decisions with your money.

What I *can* say is that I understand my own finances better now than I ever did when I was managing them "manually" — which is a generous term for what I was doing before. And I actually look forward to my 7 AM Telegram message, which is more than I can say for most of what lands in my phone at that hour.

Would my day-trading friends approve? Probably not — it's not flashy enough. But it runs while I sleep, and for someone who couldn't be bothered to check a brokerage app, that feels like a personal miracle.
